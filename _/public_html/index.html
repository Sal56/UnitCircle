The Unit Circle


Building the entire unit circle by the method described here requires that we know the first three angles of the unit circle, 30°, 45°, and 60°. We will also need to know how to convert between degrees and radians.

First we know that the circumference of the unit circle ( a circle of radius 1 ) is 2π radians. This comes from the formlua for the circumference of circle C = 2πr, the radius of a unit circle is r = 1, therefore C = 2π1
so C = 2π. A radian is, very simply, the measure of an arc, a circumference is merely the flil and complete arc of a circle. The idea of a radian is the measure of an angle by the length of its arc.

Suppose we draw a coordinate axis in x and y, then we draw a circle on this axis with a radius of one, from the x axis beginning at the origin we draw a line at an angle of 30° that intersects the unit circle,
we can get the length of the arc in radians from the relationship between the full circumference of the circle
and the degree measure of the angle. We can say that 30° is a multiplicative portion of a full circle of
360°. If we divide 360/30 = 12, so 30° is a 12th of a circle. This does not tell us anything about the length
of the arc except to say it is 1/12 th of the flil circle. We know also that the full circumference of a circle is 2π radians.
We can get the length of the arc at 30° by inferring a multiplicative relationship just as we did with degree measure.

First we have that, 2π = 360° dividing both sides by 2 we get that π = 180°. We want to know how many radians
are in 30°. We colid then next divide both sides of π = 180°
by 180°
π/180° = 180°/180°
and we get that 1° = π/180°

Next we would multiply both sides by 30° to get the number of radians in 30°, that is the length of the arc at 30° of a circle.

30° = π/180° x 30°/1
from this we get that;

30° = π/6

and 45° = π/180° x 45°/1
from this we get that;

30° = π/4 radians

and 60° = π/180° x 60°/1
from this we get that;

30° = π/3

We draw a line at 45°, and 60° and plot the three corresponding arc lengths. π/6 at 30°, π/4 at 45°, π/3 at 60°.
We can also infer that 90° = π/2 since 180° is π and mark these two points as well.
So far we have 30 = π/6, 45 = π/4, 60 = π/3, 90 = π/2 and 180 = π, we can get all the other points by this same method or we can infer some knowledge of
fractions and deduce the remaining radian measure points.
