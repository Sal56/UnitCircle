The Unit Circle

Building the entire unit circle by the method described here requires that we know the first three angles of the unit circle, 3 0 °  , 4 5 °  , and 6 0 °  . We will also need to know how to convert between degrees and radians.

First we know that the circumference of the unit circle (a circle of radius 1) is 2 π  radians. This comes from the formlua for the circumference of circle C = 2 π r  , the radius of a unit circle is r = 1  , therefore C = 2 π 1   so C = 2 π  .

A radian is, very simply, the measure of an arc, a circumference is merely the full and complete arc of a circle. The idea of a radian is the measure of an angle by the length of its arc.

Suppose we draw a coordinate axis in x and y, then we draw a circle on this axis with a radius of one, from the x axis beginning at the origin we draw a line at an angle of 3 0 °   that intersects the unit circle, we can get the length of the arc in radians from the relationship between the full circumference of the circle and the degree measure of the angle.

We can say that 3 0 °   is a multiplicative portion of a full circle of 3 6 0 °  . If we divide 3 6 0 / 3 0 = 1 2  , so 3 0 °   is a 1 2 t h   of a circle. This does not tell us anything about the length of the arc except to say it is 1 / 1 2 t h   of the full circle. We know also that the full circumference of a circle is 2 π   radians.

We can get the length of the arc at 3 0 °   by inferring a multiplicative relationship just as we did with degree measure. First we have that, 2 π = 3 6 0 °   dividing both sides by 2   we get that π = 1 8 0 °  . We want to know how many radians are in 3 0 °  . We could then next divide both sides of π = 1 8 0 °   by 1 8 0 ° π / 1 8 0 ° = 1 8 0 ° / 1 8 0 °   and we get that 1 ° = π / 1 8 0 °  

Next we would multiply both sides by 3 0 °   to get the number of radians in 3 0 °  , that is the length of the arc at 3 0 °   of a circle. 3 0 ° = π / 1 8 0 ° x 3 0 ° / 1   from this we get that; 3 0 ° = π / 6   and 4 5 ° = π / 1 8 0 ° x 4 5 ° / 1   from this we get that; 3 0 ° = π / 4   radians and 6 0 ° = π / 1 8 0 ° x 6 0 °/ 1   from this we get that; 3 0 ° = π / 3  .

We draw a line at 4 5 °  , and 6 0 °   and plot the three corresponding arc lengths. π / 6   at 3 0 °  , π / 4   at 4 5 °  , π / 3   at 6 0 °  . We can also infer that 9 0 ° = π / 2   since 1 8 0 °   is π . Next we mark these two points as well. So far we have 3 0 ° = π / 6  , 4 5 ° = π / 4  , 6 0 ° = π / 3  , 9 0 ° = π / 2   and 1 8 0 ° = π  , we can get all the other points by this same method or we can infer some knowledge of fractions and deduce the remaining radian measure points.
